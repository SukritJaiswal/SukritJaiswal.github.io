A Continuum Mechanical Approach to Model Leaf Growth

Supervisor: Prof Dr Miltos Tsiantis at Max Planck Institute for Plant Breeding Research (MPIPZ, Cologne, Germany) in the "Department of Comparative Development and Genetics"
I undertook a physics-based research project that integrated principles from solid continuum mechanics and the mechanics of biological growth to model the growth and leaf patterns of Arabidopsis thaliana. In this pursuit, I utilized FEM-based mechanical simulations and played a pivotal role in advancing the development of MorphoMechanX, a C++ software designed for the analysis of multicellular and continuous structures, particularly in the context of steady-state mechanics and growth. The core objective of my work was to address the intricate relationship between observed growth patterns and the predefined expectations for growth. I developed a two dimensional FEM model for visualising leaf primordia cells as a mesh of traingular elements and applied continuum mechanical principles to quantitatively study growth. This experience not only provided me with the opportunity to collaborate with scientists from various disciplines but also expanded the horizons of my knowledge and expertise. I also closely worked together with the "Soft & Living Matter Group" led by Prof. Apratim Chatterji.

Forbush decreases in the context of a diffusion-expansion model

Supervisor: Prof Prasad Subramanian at IISER Pune
I conducted a research project in Solar Astrophysics that specifically explored the behavior of galactic cosmic rays as they interact with Earth. This study revealed that these cosmic rays experience a temporary reduction in intensity, followed by a gradual recovery, when influenced by a solar coronal mass ejection known as Forbush decreases. I focused on analyzing Forbush decrease data, with particular attention given to the recovery phase, within the framework of a diffusion-expansion model. The primary goal was to gain insights into the diffusion coefficients governing the movement of energetic particles across magnetic fields.
